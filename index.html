The honest brief for your dates

Your brother's plan is well-structured and built the way experienced Bengali trekkers do this route — shared cars, GTA huts, and the Rinku homestay at the end. The stages are properly paced with good acclimatization built in. A few things to know before you go.

✓ Why this plan works

Day 0 at Maneybhanjan is the key smart move — you arrive rested, sort permits and your guide that evening, and start Day 1 fresh. The Tonglu → Kalipokhri → Sandakphu ascent is the classic route with ideal altitude gain each day. The descent via Srikhola (instead of backtracking) gives you a completely different landscape on the way down. This is exactly how the trek should be done.

⚠ One thing to watch on Day 4

Sandakphu → Srikhola in one push is ~16–18 km of mostly steep descent — the longest and physically hardest day. Most first-timers split it as Sandakphu → Gurdum (10 km), then Gurdum → Srikhola (6 km) the next morning. Your brother has likely assessed your fitness and knows it's doable; just start by 7 AM and if either of you is struggling at Gurdum (~2,300 m, ~10 km in), there are homestays — stay there and walk to Srikhola in the morning. Not a failure, just smart.

What makes it doable

  • No technical climbing — long uphill walk on jeep track + trail
  • Tea-house and GTA hut stays: warm beds, blankets, hot dal-bhat, no tents
  • Gradual ascent over 3 days = natural acclimatization
  • Road access at every stage — bail-out always possible

What to respect

  • Long days — 11–14 km with daypacks, 5–7 hrs moving
  • Day 4 descent is steep and knee-punishing
  • Summit nights drop to 2–8°C; bare ridge wind is bitter
  • A registered local guide is legally mandatory
🏋️ Start prepping now — ~3.5 months to go

Cardio 4–5×/week — brisk walks, stair climbs, jog. Build to a continuous 60–90 min walk comfortably. The real killer is the long descent, so train downhill specifically — stairs or any slope. Two solid months of this turns Day 4 from a grind into a pleasure.

📌 Shared car logistics — what to know

Shared Sumos from NJP fill up in the morning — be at the taxi stand by 8–9 AM to get on one. Your train arriving ~7–8 AM works perfectly for this. Vehicles go via Mirik / Rohini / Sukhia Pokhari. Year-by-year reality check

Weather in your window — what actually happens

Every operator, experienced trekker and trip report says the same thing: the monsoon tail lingers on the Singalila Ridge into late September. Eastern Himalayas have a prolonged monsoon. Here's what recent years actually looked like on the ground.

The core trade-off

Late September (26–30) = 35–40% chance of clear sunrise views, beautiful green trail, possible showers. Early October (1–4) = 60–70% chance of clear sunrise views, crisp air, drier trail. Within your 9-day window, pushing your summit night to land on October 1–3 meaningfully improves your odds of seeing the Sleeping Buddha clearly. Leave Kolkata around 28–29 September to make that happen.

2024Mixed

Late September brought lingering moisture across the ridge. Trekking groups in 25–30 Sep reported cloud-covered mornings and partial views. Those reaching Sandakphu in the last 2–3 days of September caught occasional dawn windows (~40% clear). 2023Mostly cloudy

Heavy monsoon year across Northeast India. Late September was largely overcast — most accounts from 25–30 Sep 2023 reported no clear peak views. Some brief morning windows through clouds. Early October 2023 improved fast — by Oct 2–5 the ridge was reporting sharp clear skies. Multiple Bengali group accounts confirm: "saw nothing from Sandakphu on 28–29 Sep but the trail itself was stunning."

2022Better than average

Relatively drier post-monsoon. Late September had mixed but somewhat better visibility — trekkers from 23–30 Sep 2022 report some clear mornings, especially 26–28 Sep. One Bengali group trip (27–29 Sep 2022): "saw Kanchenjunga clearly on the second morning but clouds came in fast after 7 AM." October 2022 was excellent. A decent year overall for the late-Sep window.

2019Pre-COVID benchmark

Useful baseline. Late September 2019 was transitional — some clear mornings, some cloud. Early October 2019 was widely described as excellent by multiple trek operators. One Indiahikes report from Oct 2019: "absolutely clear views of all four peaks from Sandakphu summit." Confirms the consistent pattern: late Sep is a gamble, early Oct is reliably better.

The "optimal departure" from Kolkata

To land your summit on Oct 1–3 — your best odds window — aim to depart Kolkata on 28 or 29 September. That puts Day 0 (Maneybhanjan arrival) on 29 or 30 Sep, Day 3 (Sandakphu night) on Oct 2 or 3, and sunrise on Oct 3 or 4. Fits perfectly in your window and gives you the clearest shot at the view.

🎒 The bag question

A travel holdall with no hip belt becomes genuinely painful over 6–7 hr walking days — all the weight hangs from your shoulders. The cleanest fix: hire a porter (~₹800–1,000/day, shared cost ~₹2k pp) to carry the main load, and walk with a light 20–30L daypack for water, snacks and rain layer. Solves the bag problem and keeps your energy for the trail. If you'd rather carry your own, borrow or buy a proper 40–50L rucksack with a hip belt before the trek.

👟 About your shoes

Your high-ankle, good-grip shoes will likely be fine — Sandakphu is non-technical and mostly jeep track + packed earth. The risk is the wet descent in late September: muddy trail + non-waterproof shoes = cold soaked feet all day. Test them on a 2+ hour walk now. If they stay dry and blister-free, you're good. If not, a Quechua MH500 / Forclaz pair (~₹3–4k) is the best single purchase you'll make. Either way: never wear fresh shoes on a trek — always break them in first.

Stay well up there

Altitude & safety

At ~3,636 m, mild altitude effects are possible but usually manageable because you ascend gradually over 3 days. Know the signs.

AMS warning signs

  • Persistent headache that won't shift with rest
  • Nausea, loss of appetite, dizziness
  • Unusual breathlessness at rest, poor sleep
  • Fatigue out of proportion to effort

Tell your guide immediately. The golden rule: if symptoms worsen, descend — and on this trek, you always can.

Prevention basics

  • Hydrate constantly — warm water, not cold drinks
  • Walk slow and steady; don't race ahead
  • Eat well even if appetite dips — you need the fuel
  • No alcohol on the ascent; sleep at lower halt if unsure
  • Carry Diamox only after asking a doctor
🧰 Non-negotiable checklist

Cash: no ATMs after Sukhia Pokhari — carry enough for everything plus buffer. Power bank: charging is limited and sometimes charged extra. Documents: original government photo ID + 2 passport photos for forest permit. Guide: mandatory beyond Tumling (Singalila National Park entry) — non-negotiable legally and practically. Trash: carry your wrappers out. Red panda habitat.
